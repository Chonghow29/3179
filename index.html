Water Use and Stress

Global freshwater withdrawals have increased dramatically over the past century, rising approximately six-fold since 1900, with a sharp rise from the 1950s onwards. By 2020, total withdrawals reached around 4,000 billion cubic meters, indicating a plateauing trend since 2000, reflecting growing awareness of water scarcity. Regions like North America and Asia dominate in usage, with countries like India and China exceeding 1,000 billion cubic meters each, underscoring the urgent need for sustainable water management practices to mitigate water stress.

How Is Freshwater Usage Changing Across Global Regions Over Time?

Renewable Water Resources Per Capita Over Time

Iceland leads with over 40 million cubic meters of water per capita, followed by Guyana at 19 million and Suriname at 14 million cubic meters. Papua New Guinea and Bhutan exceed 12 million cubic meters, while Canada and New Zealand have around 6 million cubic meters. This disparity in freshwater resources highlights the urgent need for sustainable water management as global pressures on these vital resources increase.

Categorized Water Stress Rankings by Country (Low to Extremely High)

Water access is a growing concern. India, the Middle East, and Mexico face extreme water stress in red, risking lives. Southern Europe and South America show high to medium stress in orange and yellow. Africa and Oceania have low stress, but grey areas in Africa signal data gaps and hidden issues. This map highlights the urgent global water crisis.

Agricultural Water Withdrawals vs. GDP Per Capita by Continent

On average, 53.6% of freshwater is used for farming, ranging from 0.15% to 98.43% in different areas. Economically, GDP per capita varies from $1,260 to $111,879, with wealthier nations often using water more efficiently. This highlights the need for sustainable agricultural practices to protect our planet while maintaining food production.

Water use of food products

Freshwater withdrawals per kilogram of food product

Animal products like cheese, prawns, and beef consume significantly more water, with cheese requiring 5,605 liters per kilogram, compared to plant-based foods such as rice and groundnuts. By opting for plant-based options, individuals can drastically reduce their water footprint and contribute to environmental sustainability.
